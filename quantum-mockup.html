02 — The Threat

Q-Day, and the fault line it draws.

Picture a giant maze; the exit is your private key. A classical computer walks the corridors one by one — there are more paths than atoms in the universe, so it never escapes. A quantum computer doesn't walk the maze: it notices the maze was built by repeating one pattern, and computes the exit directly. That is Shor's algorithm against elliptic-curve cryptography.

10,000×
The estimated cost of a quantum attack on crypto's cryptography has collapsed roughly 10,000× since 2012, paper after paper, all from the organizations that actually build the hardware.

ECC keys are ~10× smaller than RSA keys, so a smaller machine breaks them first. Crypto isn't just exposed, it's at the front of the line.

The Google–Ethereum Foundation paper (Mar 30, 2026) maps exactly what breaks: ~1,200 logical qubits and 90M Toffoli gates, executable on <500,000 physical qubits. On Bitcoin, ~6.9M BTC is vulnerable. On Ethereum, ~20.5M ETH sits in exposed top accounts, with ~$200B in stablecoins behind admin keys. But the fault line is sharp — everything sorts into two columns:

Quantum-vulnerable

Breaks under Shor's algorithm
  • ECDSA signatures (Bitcoin, Ethereum)
  • BLS consensus signatures
  • KZG commitments
  • Pairing-based SNARKs (Groth16, Plonk)
  • Optimistic rollups, sidechains

Quantum-safe

Built on hash functions, not curves
  • zk-STARK proofs
  • Hash-based commitments (BLAKE2, Poseidon)
  • Post-quantum signatures (Falcon, ML-DSA)

In the paper's own risk chart, zk-STARK is the only proving category in production today not flagged as quantum-vulnerable. Starknet is named explicitly as resistant.

04 — Starknet's Answer

Born on the right side
of the line.

Starknet enters the post-quantum era from structural strength. STARK proofs — the tech StarkWare invented and has used to secure $1.3T+ in assets and 400M+ transactions since 2021 — rely on collision-resistant hashes, not elliptic curves. The proving layer is inherently quantum-resistant. Check 6 cleared by design.

Every other major chain

Swap the engine mid-flight

The signature scheme is baked into the protocol. Changing it means a fork: every validator, client, exchange and app coordinating on a new scheme — years of work, with tail risk if you miss something. While every passenger is still on board.

Starknet

Migrate in a single transaction

Native account abstraction means every account is a smart contract that defines how its own signatures are verified. Moving to a quantum-safe scheme is just deploying a different account — a change users make in one transaction, when they choose.

Quantum-safe wallets are already live on Starknet Mainnet today. S2morrow is in production now: a post-quantum wallet using Falcon-512, implemented as a Cairo verifier, signing real transactions — and the chain needed no fork. That's check 1, live. Mainstream wallets (Ready, Braavos, Xverse) haven't migrated because they don't need to yet; when the time comes, they can — in a single transaction.

The post-quantum roadmap

Two checks cleared, one underway, the rest scoped in months, with only settlement gated on Ethereum's own timeline. Targeting end-to-end post-quantum by ~2027 — two years inside the industry's deadline.

Prof. Scott Aaronson — one of the world's foremost authorities on the capabilities and limits of quantum computers — joined StarkWare's Scientific Advisory Board in early 2026, advising on the post-quantum properties of STARKs.
